About

I am an environmental scientist investigating the interactions between the biosphere and the atmosphere. My research focus is forest ecology and palaeoenvironment. Since a very young age, I have enjoyed being in nature. This fostered a deep curiosity in me about the interactions between living organisms and their environment. At only 11 years old I decided to study plants and enter an agronomy-oriented high school. Throughout my adult life I have pursued opportunities to explore the influence of climate change in different ecosystems such as temperate and tropical high-elevation Andean forests. I also enjoy data analytics and coding!

Research Highlights

Way up high

I am investigating the potential of tree-ring isotope records for climate reconstruction in the South American Altiplano. I developed an isotopic tree-ring network from Polylepis tarapacana, the highest elevation tree-ring record in the world, located in the Altiplano in South America. The aim of this study is to build a reconstruction of climate variables such as temperature and precipitation near the tropics, as well as providing insights on ecophysiological questions related to tree-ring research.

Forest decline and extreme droughts

Climate change and forest disturbances severely impact forest ecosystem services worldwide. Throughout my research, I have explored these impacts in temperate, subtropical and tropical forests at broad spatio-temporal scales. During my PhD, I used ecological models to demonstrate that extreme drought elicits reductions in forest ecosystem productivity, including massive tree mortality in native Nothofagus pumilio forests Patagonia (Rodriguez-Caton et al., 2016, 2019). These findings demonstrated for the first time that, contrary to expectations, fast growing trees are less resilient to drought (Rodriguez-Caton et al., 2015). I also showed that the presence of woodpeckers and bark beetles are valuable indicators of tree vulnerability (Rodriguez-Caton et al., 2018).
